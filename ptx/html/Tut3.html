Subsection Exercises

1.6 Subspaces
1

Prove or disprove: The set

\begin{equation*} V := \{ p \in \Poly_2 : p(3) = 1 \} \end{equation*}

is a subspce of \(\Poly_2\text{.}\)

2.2 Linear independence
2

Consider the vector space \(V = \Fun ([0,1])\) of functions on the closed unit interval. Write down a linearly independent list containing \(4\) vectors in \(V\text{.}\)

2.3 Basis and Dimension
3

Prove or disprove: there exits a basis \(\bopen p_0, p_1, p_2, p_3 \bclose\) of \(\Poly_3 \) such that none of the polynomials \(p_0, p_1, p_2, p_3\) have degree 2.

4

Let \(W \subset \mathbb{R}^3\) be the plane orthogonal to the vector \(\ve{v} = (1,2,3)\text{,}\) as in Example 1.6.13 and Example 2.3.13. Show that \(\bopen \ve{a}, \ve{b} \bclose\) is a basis for \(W\text{,}\) where

\begin{equation*} \ve{a} = (1, 0, -\frac{1}{3}), \quad \ve{b} = (0, 1, -\frac{1}{2}). \end{equation*}
5

For each of the following, show that \(V\) is a subspace of \(\Poly_2\text{,}\) find a basis for \(V\text{,}\) and compute \(\Dim V\text{.}\)

  1. \(V = \{ p \in \Poly_2 : p(0) = 0, p(2)=0 \}\)

  2. \(V = \{ p \in \Poly_2 : \int_0^1 p(t) dt = 0 \}\)

6

Sift the list of vectors

\begin{align*} \ve{v}_1 \amp = (0,0,0), \amp \ve{v}_2 \amp = (1, 0, -1), \amp \ve{v}_3 \amp = (1, 2, 3)\\ \ve{v}_4 \amp = (3, 4, 5), \amp \ve{v}_5 \amp = (4, 8, 12), \amp \ve{v}_6 \amp = (1, 1, 0)\text{.} \end{align*}
7

Complete the following 'alternative' proof of Corollary 2.3.23.

Lemma. Suppose \(V\) is a vector space of dimension \(n\text{.}\) Then any linearly independent set of \(n\) vectors in \(V\) is a basis for \(V\text{.}\)

Proof. Let \(\basis{B} = \left\{ \ve{v}_1, \ldots, \ve{v}_n \right\}\) be a linearly independent set of vectors in \(V\text{.}\)

Suppose that \(\basis{B}\) is not a basis for \(V\text{.}\)

Therefore, \(\basis{B}\) does not span \(V\text{,}\) since ... (a)

Therefore, there exists \(\ve{v} \in V\) such that ... (b)

Now, add \(\ve{v}\) to the list \(\basis{B}\) to obtain a new list \(\basis{B}' :=\) ... (c)

The new list \(\basis{B}'\) is linearly independent because ... (d)

This is a contradiction because ... (e)

Hence, \(\basis{B}\) must be a basis for \(V\text{.}\)

8

Use the Bumping Off Proposition or the Invariance of Dimension Theorem to determine if \(\basis{B}\) is a basis for \(V\text{.}\)

  1. \(V = \Poly_2\text{,}\) \(\basis{B} = \left\{ 2 + x^2, \, 1-x, \, 1 + x - 3x^2, \, x-x^2 \right\}\)

  2. \(V = \Mat_{2,2}\text{,}\)

    \begin{equation*} \basis{B} = \left\{ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ -1 & 3 \end{bmatrix},\, \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1\\ 3 & -1 \end{bmatrix},\, \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2\\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \right\} \end{equation*}

  3. \(V = \Trig_2\text{,}\) \(\basis{B} = \left\{ \sin^2 x, \, \cos^2 x, \, 1 - \sin 2x, \, \cos 2x + 3 \sin 2x \right\}\)

  4. \(V = \Mat_{2,2}\text{,}\) \(\basis{B} = \left\{ \ve{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix},\, \ve{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ -2 & 1 \end{bmatrix},\, \ve{v}_3 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix},\, \ve{v}_4 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 3 \\ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix},\, \ve{v}_5 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\right\} \)
