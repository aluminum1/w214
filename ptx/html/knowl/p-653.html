Use the Bumping Off Proposition or the Invariance of Dimension Theorem to determine if \(\basis{B}\) is a basis for \(V\text{.}\)

  1. \(V = \Poly_2\text{,}\) \(\basis{B} = \left\{ 2 + x^2, \, 1-x, \, 1 + x - 3x^2, \, x-x^2 \right\}\)

  2. \(V = \Mat_{2,2}\text{,}\)

    \begin{equation*} \basis{B} = \left\{ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ -1 & 3 \end{bmatrix},\, \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1\\ 3 & -1 \end{bmatrix},\, \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2\\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \right\} \end{equation*}

  3. \(V = \Trig_2\text{,}\) \(\basis{B} = \left\{ \sin^2 x, \, \cos^2 x, \, 1 - \sin 2x, \, \cos 2x + 3 \sin 2x \right\}\)

  4. \(V = \Mat_{2,2}\text{,}\) \(\basis{B} = \left\{ \ve{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix},\, \ve{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ -2 & 1 \end{bmatrix},\, \ve{v}_3 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix},\, \ve{v}_4 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 3 \\ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix},\, \ve{v}_5 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\right\} \)
