Section 2.5 Change of basis

¶

Subsection 2.5.1 Coordinate vectors are different in different bases

Suppose that \(\basis{B} = \left\{ \ve{b}_1, \ve{b}_2 \right\}\) and \(\basis{C} = \left\{ \ve{c}_1, \ve{c}_2 \right\}\) are two different bases for \(\mathbb{R}^2\text{,}\) shown below:

Suppose we are given a vector \(\ve{w} \in \mathbb{R}^2\text{:}\)

We would like to compute the coordinate vector of the same vector \(\ve{w}\) with respect to the two different bases \(\basis{B}\) and \(\basis{C}\text{.}\)

For this particular \(\ve{w}\text{,}\) from Figure 2.5.1(a), we see that in the basis \(\basis{B}\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation} \ve{w} = -3 \ve{b}_1 + 2 \ve{b}_2 \qquad \therefore \, [\ve{w}]_\basis{B} = \left[\begin{array}{c} -3 \\ 2 \end{array}\right] \,\text{.}\tag{2.5.1} \end{equation}

On the other hand, in the basis \(\basis{C}\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation} \ve{w} = \ve{c}_1 - 3\ve{c}_2 \qquad \therefore \, [\ve{w}]_\basis{C} = \left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ -3 \end{array}\right]\text{.}\label{correct_exp_w}\tag{2.5.2} \end{equation}

So, the same vector \(\ve{w}\) has different coordinate vectors \([\ve{w}]_\basis{B}\) and \([\ve{w}]_\basis{C}\) with respect to the bases \(\basis{B}\) and \(\basis{C}\text{!}\)

Subsection 2.5.2 Changing from one basis to another

Now, suppose we only knew \([\ve{w}]_\basis{B}\text{,}\) the coordinate vector of \(\ve{w}\) in the basis \(\basis{B}\text{.}\) In other words, suppose we only knew that

\begin{equation*} [\ve{w}]_\basis{B} = \left[ \begin{array}{c} -3 \\ 2 \end{array}\right]\text{,} \end{equation*}

that is, \(\ve{w} = -3 \ve{b}_1 + 2 \ve{b}_2\text{.}\) How could we compute \([\ve{w}]_\basis{C}\text{,}\) the coordinate vector of \(\ve{w}\) in the basis \(\basis{C}\text{?}\)

The best way is to express each vector in the basis \(\basis{B}\) as a linear combination of the basis vectors in \(\basis{C}\text{.}\) In the next figure, the vectors \(\ve{b}_1\) and \(\ve{b}_2\) are displayed against the background of the basis \(\basis{C}\text{:}\)

We read off that:

\begin{align} \ve{b}_1 \amp = \ve{c}_1 + 3 \ve{c}_2\label{b_in_terms_of_c}\tag{2.5.3}\\ \ve{b}_2 \amp = 2 \ve{c}_1 + 3 \ve{c}_2\label{b_in_terms_of_c_2}\tag{2.5.4} \end{align}

Therefore, we compute:

\begin{align*} \ve{w} \amp = -3 \ve{b}_1 + 2 \ve{b}_2\\ \amp = -3 (\ve{c}_1 + 3 \ve{c}_2) + 2(2 \ve{c}_1 + 3 \ve{c}_2)\\ \amp = \ve{c}_1 - 3 \ve{c}_2 \end{align*}

From this we read off that

\begin{equation} [\ve{w}]_\basis{C} = \left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ -3 \end{array}\right]\label{w_in_basis_C}\tag{2.5.5} \end{equation}

which is the right answer, as we know from (2.5.2).

In fact, this calculation can be phrased in terms of matrices.

Definition 2.5.4

Let \(\basis{B} = \left\{ \ve{b}_1, \ldots, \ve{b}_n \right\}\) and \(\basis{C} = \left\{ \ve{c}_1, \ldots, \ve{c}_n \right\}\) be bases for a vector space \(V\text{.}\) The change-of-basis matrix from \(\basis{B}\) to \(\basis{C}\) is the \(n \times n\) matrix whose columns are the coordinate vectors \([\ve{b}_1]_\basis{C}, \ldots, [\ve{b}_n]_\basis{C}\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} \mat{P}_{\basis{C} \leftarrow \basis{B}} := \begin{bmatrix} \left[\begin{array}{c} \, \\ \ve{b}_1 \\ \, \end{array}\right]_\basis{C} & \left[\begin{array}{c} \,\\ \ve{b}_2 \\ \, \end{array}\right]_\basis{C} & \ldots & \left[\begin{array}{c} \, \\ \ve{b}_n \\ \,\end{array}\right]_\basis{C} \end{bmatrix}\, \text{.} \end{equation*}
Example 2.5.5

In our running example, we see from (2.5.3) and (2.5.4) that

\begin{equation*} [\ve{b}_1]_\basis{C} = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 3 \end{array} \right], \quad [\ve{b}_2]_\basis{C} = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 2 \\ 3 \end{array} \right] \, \text{.} \end{equation*}

Hence the change-of-basis matrix from \(\basis{B}\) to \(\basis{C}\) is

\begin{equation*} \mat{P}_{\basis{C} \leftarrow \basis{B}} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 3 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Before we move on, we need to recall something about matrix multiplication. Suppose you collect together \(m\) column vectors to form a matrix:

\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} \left[\begin{array}{c} \, \\ \text{C}_1 \\ \, \end{array}\right] & \left[\begin{array}{c} \,\\ \text{C}_2 \\ \, \end{array}\right] & \ldots & \left[\begin{array}{c} \, \\ \text{C}_m \\ \,\end{array}\right] \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

(For instance, our change-of-basis matrix \(\mat{P}_{\basis{C} \leftarrow \basis{B}}\) was formed in this way.) Then the product of this matrix with a column vector can be computed as follows:

\begin{equation} \begin{bmatrix} \left[\begin{array}{c} \, \\ \text{C}_1 \\ \, \end{array}\right] & \left[\begin{array}{c} \,\\ \text{C}_2 \\ \, \end{array}\right] & \ldots & \left[\begin{array}{c} \, \\ \text{C}_m \\ \,\end{array}\right] \end{bmatrix}\left[\begin{array}{c} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ \vdots \\ a_m \end{array}\right] = a_1 \left[\begin{array}{c} \, \\ \text{C}_1 \\ \, \end{array}\right] + a_2 \left[\begin{array}{c} \, \\ \text{C}_2 \\ \, \end{array}\right] + \cdots + a_m \left[\begin{array}{c} \, \\ \text{C}_m \\ \, \end{array}\right]\text{.}\label{column_vector_multiplication}\tag{2.5.6} \end{equation}
Checkpoint 2.5.6

Prove the above formula!

Solution

We check the \(i^\text{th}\) entry on the LHS using just the definition on matrix multiplation.

\begin{equation*} (\text{LHS})_i = (\text C_1)_ia_1 + \ldots + (\text C_n)_ia_n = (\text{RHS})_i \end{equation*}

and we're done!

We can now prove the following theorem.

Theorem 2.5.7 Change of basis

Suppose that \(\basis{B} = \left\{ \ve{b}_1, \ldots, \ve{b}_n\right\}\) and \(\basis{C} = \left\{ \ve{c}_1, \ldots, \ve{c}_n \right\}\) are bases for a vector space \(V\text{,}\) and let \(\mat{P}_{\basis{C} \leftarrow \basis{B}}\) be the change-of-basis matrix from \(\basis{B}\) to \(\basis{C}\text{.}\) Then for all vectors \(\ve{v}\) in \(V\text{,}\)

\begin{equation} [\ve{v}]_\basis{C} = \mat{P}_{\basis{C} \leftarrow \basis{B}} [\ve{v}]_\basis{B} \text{.}\label{change-of-basis-in-action}\tag{2.5.7} \end{equation}
Proof

Let \(\ve{v} \in V\text{.}\) Expand it in the basis \(\basis{B}\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} \ve{v} = a_1 \ve{b}_1 + a_2 \ve{b}_2 + \cdots + a_n \ve{b}_n, \mbox{i.e. } [\ve{v}]_\basis{B} = \left[\begin{array}{c} a_1 \\ \vdots \\ a_n \end{array}\right]\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then,

\begin{align*} _\basis{C} \amp = [a_1 \ve{b}_1 + \cdots + a_n \ve{b}_n]_\basis{C}\\ \amp = a_1 [\ve{b}_1]_\basis{C} + \cdots + a_n [\ve{b}_n]_\basis{C} \amp \amp (\knowl{./knowl/lin_of_coord_vectors.html}{\text{Lemma 2.4.9}})\\ \amp = \begin{bmatrix} \left[\begin{array}{c} \, \\ \ve{b}_1 \\ \, \end{array}\right] & \left[\begin{array}{c} \,\\ \ve{b}_2 \\ \, \end{array}\right] & \ldots & \left[\begin{array}{c} \, \\ \ve{b}_n \\ \,\end{array}\right] \end{bmatrix}\left[\begin{array}{c} a_1 \\ \vdots \\ a_n \end{array}\right] \amp \amp \knowl{./knowl/column_vector_multiplication.html}{\text{(2.5.6)}}\\ \amp = \mat{P}_{\basis{C} \leftarrow \basis{B}} [\ve{v}]_\basis{B} \, \text{.} \end{align*}
Example 2.5.8

In our running example, the theorem says that for any vector \(\ve{v} \in \mathbb{R}^2\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} [\ve{v}]_\basis{C} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 3 \end{bmatrix} [\ve{v}]_\basis{B} . \end{equation*}

In particular, this must hold for our vector \(\ve{w}\text{,}\) whose coordinate vector in the basis \(\ve{B}\) was:

\begin{equation*} [\ve{w}]_\basis{B} = \left[\begin{array}{c} -3 \\ 2 \end{array}\right]\text{.} \end{equation*}

So in this case, the theorem is saying that

\begin{equation*} [\ve{w}]_{\basis{C}} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \left[\begin{array}{c} -3 \\ 2\end{array}\right] = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ -3 \end{array} \right] \end{equation*}

which agrees with our previous calculation (2.5.5)!
